The role of the prior: tight, lax, and honest

Step 3 pulled a small sleight of hand. To make the posterior a simple shifted t, it used a flat prior — before seeing any data, every effect size was declared equally believable: Δ = 0.1, Δ = 3, Δ = a million. That felt humble. It isn't. It is a very strong claim that absurd effects deserve the same advance credence as realistic ones.

This page makes the prior explicit. Bayes' theorem is a multiplication —

posterior ∝ prior × likelihood

— and our unknowns come as a pair: the effect Δ and the noise σ². So the prior must be a distribution over that whole plane. The conjugate choice — the one that keeps the algebra closed — is the Normal-inverse-gamma:

Δ | σ²d ~ Normal(m₀, σ²d/κ₀) and σ²d ~ Inv-Gamma(a₀, b₀)

Its knobs are few, and each has a plain reading:

knobwhat it sayshere
m₀where you expect Δ to be, before dataa slider
κ₀how strongly — measured in pseudo-observations: your hunch enters the maths worth exactly κ₀ imaginary data pointsa slider (try the extremes)
a₀, b₀the same two roles, for the variance σ² b₀ is a slider (where you expect σ²); a₀ held fixed and gentle

The three square panels below are drawn the way Wikipedia draws this distribution: filled contours over the (Δ, σ²) plane — a teardrop of belief. On the left, the prior; on the right, the posterior; and between them, where it belongs in the multiplication, the Gaussian likelihood of the observed sample. The small green cross is the simulation's true (Δ, σ²) — in real life, the one thing you never get to see. Below the triptych, the data and the marginal over Δ: the three curves are the 1-D shadows of the three panels, on the same square scale as steps 1 and 3.

(One simplification to keep the algebra on a single screen: the model is fed the n per-pair differences dᵢ = xB,i − xA,i, each Normal(Δ, σ²d) with σ²d = 2σ².)

Try this

  1. Defaults (m₀ = 0, κ₀ = 1, n = 12). The posterior teardrop sits between prior and likelihood — much closer to the likelihood, because twelve real observations outvote one imaginary one. In the marginal, the dark curve peaks just short of the terracotta one: a whisper of healthy skepticism.
  2. Go lax: κ₀ → 0.1. The prior flattens into a vast sheet — and the posterior becomes the likelihood. Congratulations: you have rebuilt step 3. Now press new sample a few times at n = 12 and watch the posterior chase every sample across the plane. “Letting the data speak” with twelve points means letting noise speak.
  3. Skeptical but fair: m₀ = 0, κ₀ ≈ 10. The posterior is pulled toward 0 — slightly biased when the truth is 0.8 — but resampling barely moves it. With small n, trading a little bias for a lot of stability is usually a bargain; that trade is exactly what a prior buys.
  4. Tight and wrong: m₀ = −1, κ₀ = 100. A hundred imaginary observations outvote twelve real ones: the posterior parks near −1 and the green cross sits far outside the teardrop. Raise n to 100 and the data claw their way back. Tight priors are loud — be sure before you shout.
  5. Crank n to 100 and sweep κ₀ from 0.1 to 10: the posterior barely budges. Every reasonable prior is washed out by enough data. Priors matter exactly when data are scarce — which is exactly when “non-informative” is most dangerous.

The moral

  • A flat prior is not the absence of opinion. It is the opinion that preposterous effect sizes are as credible as modest ones — and with little data, that opinion leaks straight into the posterior as width and wobble.
  • κ₀ prices your conviction in the only honest currency: data. Ask “how many observations is my hunch worth?” — and notice that both answers can be wrong: 0.1 abandons the posterior to noise, 100 deafens it to evidence.
  • When n is small, weakly-informative beats non-informative; when n is large, the argument dissolves on its own — so the cases where the lax prior “plays it safe” are precisely the cases where it doesn't.
  • The prior is not a sin to hide; it is a dial to disclose. State it, vary it, and show the posterior barely moves — that is what a robust claim looks like.
