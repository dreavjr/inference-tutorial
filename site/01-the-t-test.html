How a classical test works: the t‑test

Somewhere out there are two populations — say, outcomes under treatment A and under treatment B. Each is a whole distribution of individuals (the blue and orange curves below). The truth has knobs we never observe directly: the true difference of the means (Δ) and the true variance (σ²).

What we do observe is a sample: n points from each population — the dots.

The classical recipe goes:

  1. State the null hypothesis H₀: the two populations have the same mean (Δ = 0) — against the alternative H₁: they don't.
  2. Condense the entire sample into a single number, the test statistic t = x̄B − x̄A sp √(2/n) — the observed difference of sample means, measured in units of its own estimation noise.
  3. If H₀ were true, the observed difference would still wobble around 0 from sample to sample — and in a known way: a Student's t scaled by the standard error (the dark curve). The right panel draws this on the same axis as the data, so the observed difference D̂ sits in both pictures at once. Notice how tight this band is next to the populations: the difference of means is pinned far more precisely than any single individual — and the band tightens further, like 1/√n, as the sample grows.
  4. The p-value is the probability, under H₀, of a difference at least as extreme as the one you got: the shaded two-sided tail area. (Standardised, D̂ becomes the statistic t, shown beneath the marker.)
  5. If p ≤ α (fixed here at 0.05 — the small ticks on the baseline), call the result significant and reject H₀.

Try this

  1. Drag Δ to 0. H₀ is now true. Press new sample a dozen times: D̂ dances around zero and the p-value bounces all over the place. About one press in twenty will dip below 0.05 and “discover” an effect that does not exist. That 1-in-20 is exactly α — the false-positive rate you signed up for.
  2. Δ = 0.5, n = 12: a real but modest effect, rarely significant — the test is underpowered. Now drag n to 100: same truth, yet significance almost every time. The null band tightens like 1/√n while D̂ stays put, so any nonzero difference eventually lands in the tail. Significance measures evidence, not importance.
  3. Crank σ² up and watch the null band widen and significance melt away. The test is a tug of war: signal (D̂) against noise (the band's width, se = sp√(2/n)).
  4. Drop n to 3 or 4 and watch the band turn squat and wide — and the ±t·se critical ticks slide far out: with so little data the test demands a much bigger difference before it is surprised. Raise n and the band grows so tall and tight it fades out at the top — pinned tighter than the panel can show.

The moral

  • The p-value answers exactly one question: “If there were truly no difference, how surprising would my t be?” It is a tail probability computed under H₀ — nothing more.
  • It is not the probability that H₀ is true, and a rejection is not a measure of how big or important the effect is.
  • “Reject H₀” is simply the machine stamping positive on this study. How much belief such a stamp deserves is a different question entirely — it is the subject of the next step.
