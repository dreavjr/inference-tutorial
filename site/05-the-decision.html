The Bayesian decision: from a posterior to an act

Step 4 handed us a posterior — a whole distribution over the effect Δ. But a distribution is not yet a decision. Sooner or later you must act: ship the drug, set the dose, choose B over A. A belief is only worth something once it touches a choice.

Bayesian decision theory closes that last gap with one rule: act to maximise expected utility under your posterior. It needs two ingredients — the posterior you already have, and a reward model that says how good each outcome is. We use a simple two-parameter family:

U(x) = 1 − |Δ − x|p − c · |Δ − x|

Read it plainly: if the truth is Δ and you commit to the value x, you earn the most — a full 1 — when you land exactly on the truth. Miss, and two penalties bite: a polynomial one, |Δ − x|p, and a linear one, c · |Δ − x|. For positive c the reward falls through zero at a break-even error ε — the largest miss you can still profit from — and keeps dropping past it. Negative c pushes that crossing outward and can even remove it from view. Raising p, oddly, often pushes ε outward too — for misses under one unit a higher power bites less, so a little more error is forgiven. (With c = 0, only the polynomial remains and ε sits at exactly 1.)

A belief is not yet a decision. To force the issue we ask a sharper question: given what I believe, should I place the bet at all — and if so, on what value? The bottom row answers it two ways, both committing to the same kind of bet (the peak of the belief, Δ̂) but deciding whether to bet by different rules.

On the left, your honest posterior. The Bayesian rule is to act on expected utility: average the reward — centred on your bet Δ̂ — against the whole posterior,

E[U] = ∫ reward(Δ − Δ̂) · P(Δ) dΔ

and place the bet only when that expectation is positive. The shaded gold lens is the overlap the integral measures.

On the right, the classical reading. A (1 − α) confidence interval is not a distribution, so there's nothing to average against — we draw it honestly as a flat error bar: the MLE Δ̂ at its centre, a margin of error E (the half-width) reaching either way. With no distribution to take an expectation over, the rule turns worst-case. The reward hits zero at a break-even error ε (solve 1 − εp − c·ε = 0); compare it to E — bet iff E ≤ ε, so that even the interval's worst boundary still pays. The dotted links read the reward you'd score at the MLE (the most, always 1) and at each extreme (the least, 1 − Ep − c·E).

Then each panel shows what the decision really earns. The estimated pill is the rule's own forecast — the posterior's expected reward, or the CI's min … max range — turning green when the rule says place the bet and grey when it says fold. The actual pill is the payoff once the hidden truth Δ is revealed: the true reward (the green curve, centred on Δ) read at the point you committed to — the dot, dropped from Δ̂ — or zero if you refused the bet. It turns green for a win, red for a loss, grey for a no-play. The gap between the two pills is the price of not knowing the truth.

Try this

  1. Defaults. With c = 0 the only penalty is the polynomial, so the break-even ε sits at exactly 1 — and the confidence interval fits inside it. Both rules bet, and both win: a gentle starting point where the two paradigms agree.
  2. Drop n to 3 or 4. The confidence interval balloons past ε = 1, so its worst-case boundary turns negative and the classical rule refuses, banking 0. The posterior stays compact enough to still expect a positive return — it bets, and here wins. The Bayesian's edge is sharpest when data are scarce.
  3. Crank n to 100. Both beliefs sharpen, both bet, and their actual payoffs converge near the peak reward of 1 — with enough data the rules agree again.
  4. Raise c, the linear penalty. Now every miss is charged, so the break-even ε pulls inward below 1. First the wide-interval classical rule folds; push c further and even the posterior's expectation drops below zero, so the Bayesian folds too. High enough stakes make both refuse. (With c > 0, try p as well — a higher power now widens ε, nudging both rules back toward betting.)
  5. Tighten the prior (κ₀ ↑) and shift m₀ off the truth. The posterior's bet — its peak — pulls away from the interval's midpoint, and you can watch a confident-but-wrong prior keep its estimated pill green and betting while the actual pill slides red: overconfidence, made quantitative.

The moral

  • A posterior is an input to a decision, not the decision itself. Pair it with a reward model and act on expected utility — that is the whole of Bayesian decision theory.
  • A confidence interval is not a probability distribution. The common habit of reading it as “uniform over the plausible values” is a specific, and usually worse, belief — and a decision made on it earns less.
  • The shape the posterior carries — mass piled near the estimate, thinning into the tails — is precisely what a uniform block discards, and precisely what a reward centred on the truth pays you to keep.
  • Same data, same interval width — yet a different decision, because of how honestly the uncertainty inside the interval is shaped.
  • Two numbers, never one: what a method thinks it will earn and what it actually earns. A trustworthy rule keeps the pair close; a tight wrong prior, or a mis-shaped interval, pries them apart.
