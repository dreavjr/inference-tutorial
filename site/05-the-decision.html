The Bayesian decision: from a posterior to an act

Step 4 handed us a posterior — a whole distribution over the effect Δ. But a distribution is not yet a decision. Sooner or later you must act: ship the drug, set the dose, choose B over A. A belief is only worth something once it touches a choice.

Bayesian decision theory closes that last gap with one rule: act to maximise expected utility under your posterior. It needs two ingredients — the posterior you already have, and a reward model that says how good each outcome is. We use a simple one-parameter family:

U(x) = c · (1 − |Δ − x|p) (floored at −c)

Read it plainly: if the truth is Δ and you commit to the value x, you earn the most — a full c — when you land exactly on the truth; the reward falls to zero when you miss by exactly one unit, goes negative beyond that, and bottoms out at a flat penalty −c once you're badly wrong. So the reward carves a fixed tolerance window, Δ ± 1, that you want your belief to fall inside.

A belief is not yet a decision. To force the issue we ask a sharper question: given what I believe, should I place the bet at all — and if so, on what value? The bottom row answers it two ways, both committing to the same kind of bet (the peak of the belief, Δ̂) but deciding whether to bet by different rules.

On the left, your honest posterior. The Bayesian rule is to act on expected utility: average the reward — centred on your bet Δ̂ — against the whole posterior,

E[U] = ∫ reward(Δ − Δ̂) · P(Δ) dΔ

and place the bet only when that expectation is positive. The shaded gold lens is the overlap the integral measures.

On the right, the naïve reading of a classical result: take the (1 − α) confidence interval and treat it as a uniform block — “every value inside equally plausible, everything outside impossible” — the same flat box step 1 drew. You can't take an expectation against a non-distribution, so the rule turns worst-case. The reward hits zero at a break-even error ε (solve reward(ε) = 0 — here the ±1 tolerance window). Compare it to your margin of error E, the interval's half-width: bet iff E ≤ ε, so that even the interval's worst boundary still pays. The number reported is that worst-case reward, c·(1 − Ep).

Then each panel shows what the decision really earns. The estimated pill is the rule's verdict — its number, turning green when the rule says place the bet and grey when it says fold. The actual pill is the payoff once the hidden truth Δ is revealed: the true reward (the green curve, centred on Δ) read at the point you committed to — the dot, dropped from Δ̂ — or zero if you refused the bet. It turns green for a win, red for a loss, grey for a no-play. The gap between the two pills is the price of not knowing the truth.

Try this

  1. Defaults. Both rules say bet, and both win — the interval sits well inside the ±1 window and the posterior's expectation is comfortably positive. With ample, well-behaved data the two paradigms agree.
  2. Drop n to 3 or 4. The confidence interval balloons past ±1, so its worst-case boundary turns negative and the classical rule refuses — its actual pill goes grey at 0.00, a missed opportunity. The posterior, anchored by its prior, still expects a positive return and bets — often winning. Scarce data is exactly where the worst-case reading grows timid.
  3. Crank n to 100. Both beliefs sharpen, both bet, and their actual payoffs converge near the peak reward c. With enough data the rules stop disagreeing — the same washing-out you saw with priors in step 4.
  4. Push p toward 10. The reward turns all-or-nothing — but note the break-even ε stays pinned at 1, so the classical bet/refuse verdict doesn't move; only its worst-case number does. The Bayesian side does shift: a sharp plateau rewards piling mass inside the window, so a tall posterior keeps a positive expectation a fat box would lose. Then raise c: the stakes scale, and so does the gap between what the two rules earn.
  5. Tighten the prior (κ₀ ↑) and shift m₀ off the truth. The posterior's bet — its peak — pulls away from the interval's midpoint, and you can watch a confident-but-wrong prior keep its estimated pill green and betting while the actual pill slides red: overconfidence, made quantitative.

The moral

  • A posterior is an input to a decision, not the decision itself. Pair it with a reward model and act on expected utility — that is the whole of Bayesian decision theory.
  • A confidence interval is not a probability distribution. The common habit of reading it as “uniform over the plausible values” is a specific, and usually worse, belief — and a decision made on it earns less.
  • The shape the posterior carries — mass piled near the estimate, thinning into the tails — is precisely what a uniform block discards, and precisely what a reward centred on the truth pays you to keep.
  • Same data, same interval width — yet a different decision, because of how honestly the uncertainty inside the interval is shaped.
  • Two numbers, never one: what a method thinks it will earn and what it actually earns. A trustworthy rule keeps the pair close; a tight wrong prior, or a mis-shaped interval, pries them apart.
