The Bayesian decision: from a posterior to an act

Step 4 handed us a posterior — a whole distribution over the effect Δ. But a distribution is not yet a decision. Sooner or later you must act: ship the drug, set the dose, choose B over A. A belief is only worth something once it touches a choice.

Bayesian decision theory closes that last gap with one rule: act to maximise expected utility under your posterior. It needs two ingredients — the posterior you already have, and a reward model that says how good each outcome is. We use a simple one-parameter family:

U(x) = c · (1 − |Δ − x|p) (floored at −c)

Read it plainly: if the truth is Δ and you commit to the value x, you earn the most — a full c — when you land exactly on the truth; the reward falls to zero when you miss by exactly one unit, goes negative beyond that, and bottoms out at a flat penalty −c once you're badly wrong. So the reward carves a fixed tolerance window, Δ ± 1, that you want your belief to fall inside.

The expected reward of any belief B is then the overlap of where you place your credence with where the reward lives —

E[U] = ∫ B(x) · U(x) dx

— the integral of the product of the two curves. The bottom row scores two beliefs against the same reward family. On the left, your honest posterior. On the right, the naïve reading of a classical result: take the (1 − α) confidence interval and treat it as a uniform block of probability — “every value inside is equally plausible, everything outside impossible” — the same flat box that step 1 drew, its area renormalised to 1.

Each panel reads the reward twice. The estimated reward (gold) is what you expect to earn: a reward centred on your own decision — the posterior's mode, or the interval's midpoint Δ̂ — integrated against your belief about where the truth lies. That integral is the shaded lens where belief and reward overlap. The actual reward (green) is what you really earn: a second reward, centred on the hidden truth Δ, read off at the one point you committed to — the dot, dropped from Δ̂, with no integral at all. The pill carries both numbers, and the gap between them is the price of not knowing the truth.

Try this

  1. Defaults. The posterior's estimated reward beats the CI-box's: it piles credence near the decision, where the reward is richest, while the uniform box spreads it evenly toward the interval's edges and so scores itself lower. Their actual rewards sit close — both commit to nearly the same Δ̂ — so here the posterior mostly wins by knowing its own worth.
  2. Crank n to 100. Both beliefs sharpen and the gap narrows: once the interval shrinks safely inside the window, even the crude uniform reading does fine. With enough data the shape stops mattering — the same washing-out you saw with priors in step 4.
  3. Drop n to 3 or 4. The confidence interval balloons and the box smears credence far into the red wings; the posterior, anchored by its prior, keeps more mass in the green. The Bayesian advantage is largest exactly when data are scarce — and exactly when the uniform reading is most tempting.
  4. Push p toward 10. The reward turns all-or-nothing: a flat plateau out to ±1, a cliff beyond. Now only one thing matters — how much of your belief sits inside the window — and a tall, narrow posterior and a fat box can score closer than you'd think. Then raise c: the stakes scale, and so does the gap between the two decisions.
  5. Tighten the prior (κ₀ ↑) and shift m₀ off the truth. The posterior's decision — its mode — now pulls away from the interval's midpoint, and you can watch a confident-but-wrong prior lower the actual reward even while it raises the estimated one: overconfidence, made quantitative.

The moral

  • A posterior is an input to a decision, not the decision itself. Pair it with a reward model and act on expected utility — that is the whole of Bayesian decision theory.
  • A confidence interval is not a probability distribution. The common habit of reading it as “uniform over the plausible values” is a specific, and usually worse, belief — and a decision made on it earns less.
  • The shape the posterior carries — mass piled near the estimate, thinning into the tails — is precisely what a uniform block discards, and precisely what a reward centred on the truth pays you to keep.
  • Same data, same interval width — yet a different decision, because of how honestly the uncertainty inside the interval is shaped.
  • Two numbers, never one: what a method thinks it will earn and what it actually earns. A trustworthy rule keeps the pair close; a tight wrong prior, or a mis-shaped interval, pries them apart.
